How I Actually Use AI in My Design Process

People assume using AI in design means automating things or letting it generate layouts. That’s not what I do.

AI, for me, is more like a second brain sitting next to me while I work. It doesn’t replace decisions. It challenges them. It speeds up the messy middle. It helps me think through ambiguity without getting stuck in it.

When I start a project, I’m usually staring at chaos — messy notes, half-formed stakeholder ideas, research that doesn’t fully connect yet. Instead of trying to immediately organize everything manually, I’ll dump my thinking into AI and start a conversation with it.

I’ll say something like:
“Here’s what the client wants. Here’s what users are saying. What am I missing?”

And what I’m really looking for isn’t answers — I’m looking for tension. Contradictions. Weak assumptions. Gaps in logic.

Sometimes it pushes back in ways I didn’t expect. That’s useful. It forces me to sharpen the problem before I design anything.

When I’m deep in research synthesis, AI helps me speed up the first pass of pattern recognition. I’ll feed it transcripts or clustered notes and ask, “What themes are emerging?” But I don’t blindly trust it. I cross-check everything. If it highlights something I hadn’t noticed, I go back to the source material and verify it myself.

It’s like having a research assistant who works instantly — but I’m still the one responsible for interpretation.

When I’m designing systems — especially dashboards or role-based tools — I’ll describe the structure to AI and ask it to challenge me.

“Does this navigation hierarchy make sense?”
“If you were a first-time user, where would you get confused?”
“What am I overcomplicating?”

Sometimes it exposes blind spots. Sometimes it gives bad suggestions. But even bad suggestions clarify why my approach works better. It’s less about taking its output and more about testing my own thinking against something.

Microcopy is where AI is incredibly practical. Error states. Empty states. Button labels. Confirmation messages. I’ll generate multiple variations quickly — different tones, different levels of formality — and then refine them manually.

What would normally take me five rounds of rewriting happens faster. But I still choose the final words. Tone is intentional. Personality matters.

AI helps me explore. It doesn’t decide.

When I’m designing AI-powered features themselves — like conversational tools or intelligent workflows — I use AI to simulate real usage. I’ll throw unpredictable prompts at it. I’ll try to break it. I’ll look for hallucinations or ambiguity.

That process has made me much more cautious about how AI is presented to users. I care deeply about guardrails, transparency, and making sure users understand what the system is doing. Designing with AI every day has made me more critical of AI — not more trusting of it.

Even when I write case studies or documentation, AI plays a role. I’ll give it messy drafts and ask it to structure them more clearly. Sometimes it reframes something in a way that makes the impact sharper. But the story is mine. The thinking is mine. It’s just helping me articulate it better.

There are also moments when I feel stuck creatively. Instead of forcing myself into one direction, I’ll ask AI something uncomfortable like:

“What’s the opposite way to design this?”
“If this had to be radically simpler, what would disappear?”
“What would this look like if it were designed for someone who hates dashboards?”

Not because I’ll use those ideas directly. But because they expand the space I’m thinking in. They prevent me from designing on autopilot.

What I don’t do is let AI decide for me.

It doesn’t replace user interviews.
It doesn’t validate strategy.
It doesn’t make ethical calls.
It doesn’t finalize features.

It accelerates exploration. It challenges assumptions. It helps me move faster without becoming careless.

If anything, using AI daily has made me more intentional as a designer. I question outputs more. I think harder about clarity. I test more scenarios before committing.

AI hasn’t reduced the depth of my process.

It’s increased the range of it.
