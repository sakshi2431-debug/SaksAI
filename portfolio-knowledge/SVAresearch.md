🧠 Context & Challenge

During my time working on SVA’s digital research guides, I discovered that the School of Visual Arts had a rich repository of online academic resources, but students struggled to navigate it effectively. Research guides existed, but users didn’t use them as intended — not because the content was bad, but because the experience wasn’t aligned with how users naturally browse and make decisions online.

The SVA Library was also overwhelmed with repetitive questions from students who couldn’t find what they needed on their own. This revealed a core UX problem: the tools weren’t helping students discover and access content efficiently.

🎯 Problem Statement

How might we make it easier for students to find the right resources in SVA’s research guides?
The existing search and navigation patterns caused confusion, information overload, and inefficient search behaviors — students often resorted to guesswork rather than systematic exploration.

👥 Research Approach

I worked with a team of SVA students to evaluate the experience through a three-pronged research methodology:

Eye-Tracking for Task Completion

System Usability Scale (SUS) Surveys

Heuristic Evaluation & Usability Testing
This combination helped me directly observe how users look, think, and behave — not just what they say they do.

🔍 Key Insights
🔎 Insight 1 — Navigation Patterns Are Not What You Think

Participants didn’t use the site like a typical research portal. Instead of diving into paragraphs, they scanned for visual cues, headings, and familiar keywords. Dense blocks of text caused them to skip content entirely.

🔎 Insight 2 — Search Was Underperforming

Even though students preferred the search bar, it often failed to return useful results for realistic queries like “history of American fashion.” Many simply gave up and tried to eyeball their way through resource lists.

🔎 Insight 3 — Layout Increased Cognitive Load

The three-column structure forced users to make unnecessary decisions (left vs right vs center flow), which slowed down task completion and reduced focus.

🔎 Insight 4 — Visual Hierarchy Was Weak

Small labels and undifferentiated text meant users struggled to parse sections — they got lost scrolling back and forth without a clear anchor point.

🧪 Testing Metrics

83% task success rate for finding relevant resources

75% identified keywords and preview images as helpful

SUS scores improved insights into usability bottlenecks
These metrics grounded our recommendations in both behavioral and subjective evidence.

💡 Design Recommendations

Based on synthesized data from eye-tracking, quotes, click patterns, and SUS scores, I formulated recommendations in three focus areas:

🎯 1. Improve Scanability

Students scan first, read second. So I recommended:

Larger logos and icons

Bold, descriptive headings

Keyword tags for resource previews
These help users spot relevant content at a glance.

🎯 2. Strengthen Search Functionality

A better search should:

Support complex phrases (e.g., “history of American fashion”)

Auto-correct typos

Return broader semantically relevant results
This minimizes frustration and empowers users to trust the search tool again.

🎯 3. Structure Content to Reduce Cognitive Load

Instead of sprawling columns:

Use tabs to filter relevant sections

Group related resources under clear headers
This gives users anchor points to reference rather than forcing them to scroll endlessly.

📣 Client Feedback

“Great recommendations from this group for reducing item and database descriptions on our guides, and for a better way to organize content on the exemplary guide, Design Archives. We can use these suggestions to apply system-wide updates to all of our guides.”
— Digital Services Librarian, SVA Library

🧠 What I Learned

This project sharpened my skills in translating raw behavioral data into clear design narratives. Seeing exactly where users looked — and where they didn’t — taught me that good UX research isn’t just about what users want, but how they actually behave.

I also learned how to:

Combine qualitative quotes with quantitative eye data

Craft solutions that are practical and measurable

Communicate research insights in ways stakeholders can act on confidently
