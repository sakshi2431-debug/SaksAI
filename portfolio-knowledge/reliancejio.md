🧠 Context & Challenge

When I first engaged with Reliance Jio’s digital ecosystem, it became immediately clear that users weren’t behaving like passive consumers — they were overwhelmed. The ecosystem had massive scale, but with scale came confusion, inconsistency, and unclear decision points. Users were dropping off not because the products were broken, but because the flow, governance, and information architecture lacked clarity.

My directive was to unlock comprehension and reduce cognitive load at every step, especially in high-stakes flows like onboarding, payment choices, and customer self-service. (sakshirane.com
)

🎯 Problem Statement

“How might we redesign Reliance Jio’s user experience to be more intuitive, more transparent, and more empowering — while aligning with business constraints?”

Users were:

Confused by plan options

Unsure about value differentiation

Skeptical of hidden fees

Frustrated with unclear next steps
This wasn’t a visual problem — it was a mental model problem. (sakshirane.com
)

👥 Research Approach

To address this, I used a mixed-methods research design combining:

User Interviews & Surveys
— Capturing perceived pain points and expectations.

Heuristic Evaluation
— Auditing existing screens for cognitive friction, inconsistency, and mental overhead.

Journey Mapping
— Pinpointing where confusion peaked, where abandonments happened, and where users needed guidance instead of guesswork. (sakshirane.com
)

🔍 Key Insights
🔎 Insight 1 — Plan Overload Causes Paralysis

Users faced endless rows of plans with similar names and unclear differentiation:

“Unlimited PLUS”

“4G Max Value”

“Supreme Chat Discounts”
These names felt like marketing noise, not meaningful decision signals. (sakshirane.com
)

Impact: Users dropped off here faster than any other section.

🔎 Insight 2 — Users Wanted Why It Matters

Plan tiers rarely explained why one mattered over the other. Users kept asking:

“Is this really worth paying ₹30 more? What do I actually get?”

They needed benchmark value propositions rather than marketing buzzwords. (sakshirane.com
)

🔎 Insight 3 — Hidden Steps Kill Trust

Users were consistently surprised by:

Billing breakdowns

Add-on charges

Data speed caps
This reduced trust — and trust is the bedrock of telecom loyalty, where switching is easy. (sakshirane.com
)

🧪 What the Data Said

62% of participants misinterpreted plan benefits

78% felt uncertainty about billing

53% wanted clearer comparisons across plans
These numbers didn’t just validate intuition — they highlighted where specific design decisions needed to shift. (sakshirane.com
)

💡 Solution Themes
1. Model-Based Plan Comparison

Instead of stacked cards with marketing titles, I introduced:

Clear value tiers

Quantified benefits (data, calls, OTT credits)

Example-based scenarios
This gave users context, e.g., “If you watch 1 movie / week, this plan saves you ₹X.” (sakshirane.com
)

2. Transparent Billing Breakdown

Rather than surprise fees:

I proposed a line-item view

Labels like “GST • Add-On • Data Overages”

Expected total cost calc at each step
This shift reduced cognitive load and increased trust in the checkout flow. (sakshirane.com
)

3. Onboarding Usability Improvements

Onboarding questions were causing confusion because they didn’t feel personalized:

“What will you use most?” → vague
I rewrote this as task-oriented questions:

“Do you watch videos daily?”

“Do you stream music?”
This aligned plan suggestions with real user intent, not product spec language. (sakshirane.com
)

🧠 What I Learned

This project grounded my belief that confusion is not a UI problem — it’s a mental model problem. Great UX isn’t just about aesthetics; it’s about clarity, predictability, and trust. (sakshirane.com
)

I also learned:

Users value explainability over novelty

Comparisons should be contextual, not abstract

Transparency directly affects perception of fairness

📣 Business Impact

By aligning clarity with conversion, the redesigned flows aimed to:

Reduce drop-offs in plan selection

Increase confidence in checkout

Lower post-purchase support tickets
These outcomes matter because retention in telecom is fundamentally tied to user certainty, not just pricing.
